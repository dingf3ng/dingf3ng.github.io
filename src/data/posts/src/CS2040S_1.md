<div class="minipage">
<p><strong>Let’s learn binary search!</strong><br />
<br />
Imagine you have a phone book and you need to find "Smith, John". You
wouldn’t start at "Aardvark" and read every name, would you?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Of course not. I’d open it somewhere in the middle, probably near the
’S’ section.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Exactly! And that’s the core idea of binary search. But it has one
<strong>critical</strong> rule.<br />
<br />
The phone book works because all the names are...</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>...sorted. Alphabetically.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Precisely. Binary search <em>only</em> works on a
<strong>sorted</strong> list. If the list is unsorted, you’re stuck
checking every single item.<br />
<br />
Let’s try it. Here is our sorted list:<br />
<code>[2, 5, 8, 12, 16, 23, 38, 56, 72]</code><br />
<br />
We want to find the number <code>23</code>.</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Okay, so I "open the book to the middle."<br />
<br />
The list has 9 items. The middle one is the 5th item, which is
<code>16</code>.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Great. Now we ask a simple question: Is the number we’re looking for
(<code>23</code>) <em>equal to</em>, <em>less than</em>, or <em>greater
than</em> the middle number (<code>16</code>)?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>It’s greater. <code>23 &gt; 16</code>.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Right. And since the list is sorted, what does that tell us?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>It means <code>23</code> <em>must</em> be in the second half... the
part to the right of <code>16</code>.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>You got it! So we can completely <strong>discard</strong> the entire
first half of the list, including <code>16</code>.<br />
<br />
Our new, smaller list to search is:<br />
<code>[23, 38, 56, 72]</code></p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Ah! And now we just... repeat the process?</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Exactly. What’s the middle of this new list?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>It has 4 items. There’s no single "middle." Does it matter if I pick
<code>38</code> or <code>56</code>?</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Good question. You just need to be consistent. Let’s pick the "left"
middle element,<a href="#fn1" class="footnote-ref" id="fnref1"
role="doc-noteref"><sup>1</sup></a> which is <code>38</code>.<br />
<br />
Now, compare: Is <code>23</code> greater or smaller than
<code>38</code>?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>It’s smaller.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>So... what do we discard?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>We discard <code>38</code> and everything to its
<em>right</em>.<br />
<br />
Our new list is just:<br />
<code>[23]</code></p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>And we repeat one last time. What’s the middle of
<code>[23]</code>?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>...it’s just <code>23</code>.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Is <code>23</code> equal to our target?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Yes! We found it. That was much faster than checking from the
start.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>What if we had been looking for <code>24</code>?</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>Let’s see. We’d get to the <code>[23]</code> step.<br />
We’d ask: Is <code>24</code> greater or smaller than
<code>23</code>?<br />
It’s greater. So I’d discard <code>23</code>...<br />
<br />
...and my list would be empty.</p>
</div>
<p><br />
<br />
</p>
<div class="minipage">
<p>And that’s how you know the number isn’t in the list at all.<br />
<br />
By repeatedly "halving" the search space, you find items incredibly
fast. That’s why it’s a cornerstone of computer science!</p>
</div>
<div class="minipage">
<p>That makes perfect sense. Sorted list, check the middle, discard one
half, repeat. Simple.</p>
</div>
